# Government Use of Open Source Software

> Last updated: 2026-03-15

## Summary

Governments worldwide are increasingly adopting open source software for procurement, development, and digital infrastructure. Government OSS policies range from "permit" (allowing OSS alongside proprietary options) to "prefer" (favoring OSS by default) to "mandate" (requiring OSS in specific contexts). Legal considerations include procurement law, security requirements, sovereignty concerns, and compliance with open source licenses.

## Key Principles

- Government use of OSS is generally subject to the same license terms as private-sector use
- Government employees' work is typically in the public domain (US) or Crown copyright (UK/Commonwealth)
- Procurement regulations may create additional requirements for OSS adoption
- Security certification requirements (FedRAMP, Common Criteria) apply regardless of license
- "Sovereign tech" movements frame OSS as a matter of national digital sovereignty

## US Federal Policy

### Key Policy Documents
- **OMB Memorandum M-16-21 (2016):** "Federal Source Code Policy" — requires agencies to release at least 20% of new custom-developed code as open source (pilot program)
- **Code.gov:** Repository of federal government open source projects
- **NIST guidance:** Various publications on OSS security, SBOM requirements, and supply chain risk management
- **CISA guidance:** Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency guidance on securing open source software in critical infrastructure

### Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR)
- OSS is treated as COTS (commercial off-the-shelf) software for procurement purposes
- Agencies can use OSS without special procurement justification
- However, support, maintenance, and integration services may still be procured through standard FAR processes

### Department of Defense
- DoD has been a significant advocate for OSS use
- DoD CIO guidance explicitly permits and encourages OSS
- Specific guidance on evaluating OSS for security and suitability

## EU Policy

### EU Open Source Strategy (2020-2023)
- "Think Open" strategy promoting OSS in EU institutions
- OSPOs (Open Source Program Offices) established in EU Commission
- EU-FOSSA (Free and Open Source Software Auditing) program funded security audits of critical OSS

### Member State Policies
- **France:** Strong OSS policy; government promotes and contributes to OSS (code.gouv.fr)
- **Germany:** Sovereign Tech Fund provides direct funding to critical OSS projects
- **Italy:** Guidelines for acquisition and reuse of software in public administration
- **Netherlands:** "Comply or explain" policy for OSS in government IT

## Practical Guidance

1. **OSS is COTS.** In the US, open source software qualifies as commercial software for procurement purposes. No special justification is needed.

2. **License compliance still applies.** Government agencies must comply with open source licenses — including copyleft source code obligations if they distribute modified software.

3. **Government contributions may be public domain.** In the US, works created by federal employees in the course of their duties are not subject to copyright (17 U.S.C. § 105). Code written by government employees may be in the public domain, which can create complications with copyleft licenses that expect copyright ownership.

4. **Security requirements are independent.** Using OSS doesn't waive security certification requirements (FedRAMP, FISMA, Common Criteria). OSS must meet the same security standards as proprietary software.
