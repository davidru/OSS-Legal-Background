# Sovereign Open Source Strategies

> Last updated: 2026-03-15

## Summary

A growing number of countries are developing national strategies around open source software as a component of digital sovereignty — the ability to exercise control over a nation's digital infrastructure, data, and technological capabilities without dependence on foreign entities. Open source is seen as a tool for reducing vendor lock-in, building domestic technical capacity, and ensuring security through transparency.

## Key Principles

- Digital sovereignty is not about isolationism — it's about strategic independence and choice
- OSS enables sovereignty by providing access to source code, eliminating dependence on foreign vendors
- Some sovereignty initiatives focus on creating national alternatives to foreign platforms (Gitee vs. GitHub)
- Others focus on contributing to and supporting global open source projects
- Geopolitical tensions (US-China tech competition, EU digital autonomy) are accelerating sovereignty-driven OSS policies

## Country Strategies

### European Union
- **Digital Sovereignty as stated policy:** EU explicitly frames open source as a tool for digital sovereignty
- **Sovereign Tech Fund (Germany):** Direct government funding to critical OSS projects
- **Gaia-X:** European cloud infrastructure initiative with OSS components
- **EU OS (operating system):** Discussions about EU-sovereign operating system alternatives
- **CRA and regulatory approach:** EU regulation creates incentives for transparent (OSS) software

### China
- **National priority:** China has designated OSS as strategically important for reducing dependence on US technology
- **OpenAtom Foundation:** China's primary OSS foundation, government-supported
- **Gitee:** Chinese GitHub alternative, subject to government content requirements
- **OpenHarmony/OpenEuler:** Chinese OS projects positioning as alternatives to Android/Linux distributions
- **Regulatory environment:** Chinese courts enforce OSS licenses; government promotes OSS governance standards

### India
- **India Stack:** Government digital infrastructure with significant open source components
- **Digital Public Goods:** India positions its government-developed software as digital public goods
- **Growing ecosystem:** India is a major contributor to global OSS projects

### Brazil
- **Software Público Brasileiro:** Government platform for sharing public-sector developed software
- **Marco Legal da IA:** AI regulation discussions include OSS considerations
- **Long history:** Brazil has promoted government use of OSS since the early 2000s

## Geopolitical Considerations

### US-China Technology Competition
- Export controls (Entity List, chip restrictions) have accelerated China's investment in indigenous OSS alternatives
- Open source is caught in the middle: is it a tool for collaboration or a vector for technology transfer?
- RISC-V (open source hardware ISA) is a focal point — China is investing heavily in RISC-V as an alternative to ARM/x86

### OSS as Neutral Ground
- Open source projects that operate as genuinely global, multi-stakeholder communities can serve as neutral ground for technology collaboration
- However, participation in OSS governance by entities from rival nations creates complex political dynamics
- "It's Model UN, not litigation" — but the stakes are real

## Practical Guidance

1. **Sovereignty and open source are natural allies.** Access to source code is a prerequisite for digital sovereignty. You cannot be sovereign if your infrastructure depends on opaque foreign software.

2. **But sovereignty ≠ isolation.** The best sovereignty strategy is participating in global open source communities while building domestic capability — not creating national alternatives that fragment the ecosystem.

3. **Export controls create asymmetries.** US export controls affect the ability of some countries to participate in certain open source projects, creating incentives for alternative ecosystems.
