# Open Source and Competition Law

> Last updated: 2026-03-15

## Summary

Open source software intersects with antitrust and competition law in complex ways. Collaborative development among competitors raises antitrust questions, but open source development has generally been recognized as pro-competitive — it lowers barriers to entry, promotes interoperability, and enables innovation by reducing the cost of foundational software.

## Key Principles

- Collaborative open source development among competitors is generally pro-competitive
- Antitrust risk arises from the conduct of participants, not from open source development itself
- Standard-setting context creates heightened antitrust scrutiny (see also [Standards and OSS](standards-and-oss.md))
- Foundation meetings where competitors gather require antitrust compliance measures
- Open source can be BOTH a tool of competition and a mechanism for collaboration

## Case Law

### Wallace v. IBM Corp., 467 F.3d 1104 (7th Cir. 2006)
- **Citation:** 467 F.3d 1104 (7th Cir. 2006)
- **Holding:** Dismissed antitrust challenge to the GPL. Plaintiff argued that the GPL constituted an illegal price-fixing conspiracy (fixing the price at zero). Judge Easterbrook held that zero-price distribution promotes rather than restricts competition.
- **Significance:** The only appellate decision directly addressing antitrust claims against open source licensing. Confirms that open source licensing is not inherently anticompetitive.
- **Key Quote:** "If the GPL and open-source licensing come to dominate the market for operating systems, it will be because no one wants to buy proprietary software. That outcome would be a triumph of competition, not a violation of the Sherman Act."

## Key Antitrust Risks in Open Source Contexts

### Foundation/Consortium Meetings
- Competitors meeting in foundation governance bodies must avoid discussions of:
  - Pricing or pricing strategies
  - Market allocation or customer division
  - Boycotts of specific companies or products
  - Non-public competitive information
- Best practice: Review antitrust guidelines at the start of every meeting

### Exclusionary Conduct
- Using open source governance to exclude competitors (e.g., designing standards or APIs that disadvantage rivals) could raise antitrust concerns
- Patent policies that effectively lock out competitors
- Licensing choices designed to disadvantage specific business models

### Market Dominance and Open Source
- A dominant company open-sourcing a product to undermine competitors' revenue could raise concerns (predatory pricing theory), though Wallace v. IBM makes this difficult to sustain
- Using open source contributions to gain market intelligence about competitors
- Leveraging open source ecosystem control for competitive advantage

## Commentary & Analysis

### EU Competition Law Perspective
- The European Commission has generally viewed open source favorably as promoting interoperability and competition
- DG Competition has not brought any enforcement actions against open source practices
- The EU's approach to platform regulation (Digital Markets Act) intersects with open source as a tool for interoperability compliance

### Herbert Hovenkamp on Technology and Antitrust
- Leading antitrust scholar has noted that collaborative standard-setting and open source development are generally pro-competitive
- The antitrust analysis should focus on effects, not form — collaboration that promotes output and innovation is lawful

## Practical Guidance

1. **Antitrust guidelines at every meeting.** Any gathering where competitors are present should begin with a reminder of antitrust rules.

2. **Open source governance is not a license to collude.** Foundation meetings can discuss technical standards and specifications, but not pricing, market allocation, or boycotts.

3. **Document decisions.** Governance decisions that could be perceived as exclusionary should be documented with the technical or policy rationale.

4. **Compete on execution, not governance.** Use open source governance to promote the common good, not to disadvantage specific competitors.
